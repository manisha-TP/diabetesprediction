Key Diabetes Risk Factors

Age

Risk increases with age, especially after 45 years. The body's ability to process glucose may decline over time.

Body Mass Index (BMI)

Higher BMI (especially >25) increases insulin resistance. Excess weight affects how your body uses insulin.

Family History

Genetic factors play a significant role. Having a parent or sibling with diabetes increases your risk.

Common Symptoms
  • Frequent Urination: High blood sugar causes kidneys to work harder
  • Excessive Thirst: Body tries to replace fluids lost through urination
  • Unusual Fatigue: Cells can't get enough glucose for energy
  • Blurred Vision: High blood sugar affects eye lens shape

Understanding Diabetes

Type 1 Diabetes

Autoimmune condition where the body doesn't produce insulin. Usually develops in childhood.

Type 2 Diabetes

Body doesn't use insulin effectively. Most common type, often preventable through lifestyle changes.

Gestational

Develops during pregnancy. Usually resolves after delivery but increases future risk.

Prevention Strategies
  • Maintain healthy weight
  • Regular physical activity
  • Balanced diet low in sugar
  • Regular health screenings
  • Avoid smoking
  • Limit alcohol consumption
  • Manage stress effectively
  • Get adequate sleep

When to Seek Medical Attention

Consult a healthcare provider if you experience multiple diabetes symptoms, have high risk factors, or if this assessment indicates moderate to high risk. Professional testing includes blood glucose, HbA1c, and other comprehensive evaluations.
